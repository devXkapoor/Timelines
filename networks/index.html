ERA 0

Before Computers — The Idea of a Network

Antiquity → 1830s
📡

What is "communication"? Starting from absolute zero

The concept of transmitting information across distance
▾

The fundamental problem

Imagine two people standing in a field. Person A wants to tell Person B something — for example, "the enemy army is approaching from the north." Person A can shout. Person B can hear. This works if they are close enough.

But what if they are 50 kilometres apart? Shouting does not work. And so, for thousands of years, humans faced one of the most fundamental engineering problems in all of history:

The core problem: How do you send a piece of information from one place to another, faster than a person can physically travel?

Every single concept in computer networking — every wire, every protocol, every router, every cell tower — is an attempt to answer this exact question. The computers are new. The problem is ancient.

What is "information"?

Before we can transmit information, we need to understand what information actually is. Here is the simplest possible definition:

Information is the difference between two possibilities. If I flip a coin and I already know it will always land heads, telling you "it landed heads" gives you zero information — you already knew. But if it could be heads or tails, and I tell you which one it is, I have given you information: I have reduced your uncertainty.

This idea — that information is the reduction of uncertainty — was formalised by Claude Shannon in 1948 (we will get there). But the instinct was always there in human communication: every message we send is meant to tell the receiver something they did not already know.

What is a "signal"?

To transmit information, you need to encode it into something physical that can travel. That physical thing is called a signal. A signal is any change in a physical quantity that carries meaning.

Examples of signals humans have always used:

Sound (voice, drums)
Variations in air pressure travel as sound waves. Limited range — a few km at most.
Light (fire, mirrors)
Visible light travels in straight lines. Beacons on hilltops could relay signals hundreds of km.
Physical (runners, riders)
A human carrying a message. Reliable, but only as fast as a horse.
Smoke, flags, semaphore
Visual signals that can encode simple messages over long distances in daylight.
🏔️

The first networks — relay chains and optical semaphore

1700s–1830s: the first multi-node communication systems
▾

The relay principle

Here is a key insight that is still at the heart of modern networking: if you cannot go the full distance in one step, go partway, and hand off to someone who goes the next part.

This is called relaying. A relay is an intermediate station that receives a signal and re-transmits it. The chain of relay stations together forms a network.

Analogy: Imagine you want to send a letter from Delhi to Mumbai. Rather than finding a single person who will walk the entire distance, you find someone who goes to Pune, who hands it to someone going to Pune station, who puts it on a train… The letter "hops" from node to node. Each hop is a relay.

The optical telegraph — Chappe, 1791

In 1791, a French engineer named Claude Chappe built the first true long-distance communication network: the optical telegraph, also called the Chappe telegraph or "semaphore telegraph."

Here is how it worked: a tall tower had two large wooden arms at the top. An operator could move these arms into different positions. Each combination of positions was assigned a meaning — letters, numbers, or military codes. An operator at the next tower, 10–15 km away, watched through a telescope and when he saw a new arm position, he copied it exactly on his own tower. And so on, down the chain.

Tower 1 Tower 2 Tower 3 (Paris) (Meudon) (Chaville) |/ ←arms |/ |/ | [encode] copy | [relay] copy | | | | Operator Operator Operator types watches watches message + copies + copies into arms immediately immediately Signal travels: ──────────────────────────────────────► 10-15 km per hop, many hops, ~1 word/minute

By 1844, France had 556 stations covering 5,000 km. A message could travel from Paris to Bordeaux (600 km) in under 10 minutes. This was the first example of:

1. A network topology — a map of how nodes are connected to each other.
2. Switching — routing a message through intermediate nodes.
3. Encoding — turning a message into a signal that can be transmitted.

All three concepts are still central to computer networking today. The hardware changed. The concepts did not.

ERA 1

The Electric Telegraph

1830s–1900s — Electricity becomes a medium for information
⚡

Electricity as a signal medium — why it changed everything

From visible light to invisible electrons
▾

What is electricity? (From absolute zero)

Everything in the universe is made of atoms. Atoms contain a nucleus in the centre, with electrons orbiting around it. Electrons carry a negative electrical charge. In certain materials — metals in particular — some electrons are loosely attached to their atoms and can move freely from atom to atom.

When you apply a force (a voltage) at one end of a metal wire, free electrons begin flowing from one atom to the next, all the way along the wire. This flow of electrons is called electric current. It travels at close to the speed of light — roughly 300,000 km per second.

Key insight: Light takes about 8 minutes to travel from the Sun to Earth. Electricity in a wire crosses a continent in milliseconds. For the first time in history, information could travel faster than any physical object.

How electricity becomes a signal

To carry information, you need to vary the electricity in some way. The simplest variation is on/off: current flowing (ON = 1) vs. no current (OFF = 0). This is the foundation of all digital communication.

Samuel Morse and Alfred Vail developed a system in the 1830s–1840s where they varied the duration of the current pulse:

SHORT pulse = "dot" (·) LONG pulse = "dash" (—) Letter A = · — Letter B = — · · · Letter S = · · · Letter O = — — — SOS = · · · — — — · · · The operator presses a key (closes the circuit) for a short time (dot) or long time (dash).

This is called Morse code. It is an encoding scheme — a way of representing letters and numbers using a simple physical signal (current on/off). The receiver at the other end hears clicks or sees marks on paper, and decodes them back into letters.

The first telegraph network — 1844 onward

On 24 May 1844, Morse sent the first official telegraph message from Washington DC to Baltimore: "What hath God wrought?" — a 60 km wire. Within 20 years, telegraph wires spanned continents.

🔧

Engineering problems in the telegraph network

Signal degradation, multiplexing, routing — problems that still exist today
▾

Signal degradation — why signals weaken

Wires are not perfect. As electrical current travels through a wire, some energy is lost to heat (resistance) and some leaks out sideways (capacitance). After enough distance, the signal becomes too weak to detect reliably. This is called signal attenuation.

The solution was the repeater — an intermediate station that detects the weakened signal, understands it, and re-sends it at full strength. A human operator was the first repeater. Later, electrical amplifiers replaced human operators.

This problem never went away. Fiber optic cables under the Atlantic Ocean today have repeater stations every 50–80 km to boost the light signal. The principle is identical to what Morse needed.

Multiplexing — sharing one wire for many conversations

Building one wire per pair of communicating parties is expensive. Telegraph companies needed a way to send multiple messages on the same wire simultaneously. This challenge is called multiplexing — combining multiple signals on one channel.

Emile Baudot (1870s) invented a solution: send signals at slightly different rhythms, and at the receiver, a synchronized mechanism separates them back out. This is called time-division multiplexing: different signals take turns using the wire at different moments in time.

Routing — how does a message know where to go?

The telegraph network quickly became a web of wires, not just a single line. When a message arrived at a central office, a human operator had to decide: which outgoing wire should this go on to reach the destination? This human decision is called routing.

The telegraph companies built dedicated switching offices — buildings full of operators whose job was to receive incoming messages and re-transmit them on the correct outgoing line toward the destination. This is called store-and-forward switching: receive the whole message, store it briefly, then forward it on.

Analogy: A postal sorting centre does exactly this. The parcel arrives, the sorter reads the address, and sends it to the right outgoing truck. Computer routers do the same thing, but with electronic packets instead of parcels, and in microseconds instead of hours.
📞

The telephone and circuit switching (1876)

Continuous signals, dedicated connections, and the first voice network
▾

Analog signals vs digital signals

The telegraph used discrete signals — ON or OFF, dot or dash. This is called a digital signal: it can only take specific, distinct values.

Human voice is completely different. Sound is continuous vibration in air — pressure that rises and falls smoothly over time. To transmit voice, you need to transmit a signal that also rises and falls continuously. This is called an analog signal — it varies continuously, mirroring (being analogous to) the original sound wave.

Alexander Graham Bell's telephone (1876) converted the continuous pressure of sound waves into continuous variations in electrical current, sent that varying current down a wire, and at the other end converted it back to sound using a speaker. The electrical wave was an analog copy of the sound wave.

Circuit switching — a new kind of network

The telephone network introduced a fundamentally different model from the telegraph: circuit switching.

When you dial a telephone number, a physical electrical path — a circuit — is set up end-to-end between you and the person you are calling. Every switch along the path physically connects your wire to theirs. This path is yours exclusively for the duration of the call. When you hang up, the circuit is torn down and the switches return to their neutral state.

Circuit switching: Caller ──[SW1]──[SW2]──[SW3]── Receiver | | | dedicated physical path reserved ENTIRELY for this one call even during silence! SW = telephone exchange switching matrix (a mechanical crossbar switch, later electronic)

Advantages: Guaranteed bandwidth. No delays. Perfect for voice — continuous, real-time conversation.
Disadvantage: Wasteful. If both people are silent, the circuit is still entirely reserved. You are paying for a dedicated road whether you are using it or not.

The telephone system, which grew into a global network of billions of calls per day, was built entirely on circuit switching. This will become very important when we explain why the internet chose a completely different approach.

ERA 2

Early Computing — Isolated Islands

1945–1960s — Computers exist, but do not talk to each other
🖥️

The isolated computer problem

Why the first computers didn't communicate at all
▾

What computers were in the 1940s–1950s

The first computers — ENIAC (1945), the Manchester Baby (1948), the UNIVAC (1951) — were enormous physical machines. ENIAC filled an entire room (167 square metres), weighed 27 tonnes, and consumed 150 kilowatts of electricity. There was exactly one of it.

Computers at this stage existed in total isolation. Data went in as punched cards or paper tape. Results came out on a printout. There was no concept of connecting computers to each other, because:

1. There were only a handful of computers in the entire world, owned by universities and government agencies.
2. The idea of real-time communication between machines had not been conceived yet.
3. The existing telephone network used analog signals; computers used discrete digital signals — they were incompatible by default.

Batch processing — the first computing model

In early computing, users did not interact with the computer directly in real time. Instead, they wrote a program on punched cards, gave the cards to an operator, and came back hours later to collect the printed output. This is called batch processing: jobs are collected in batches and processed one after another.

There was no need for networking in this model — data came in physically, results left physically. The computer was an island.

Time-sharing — the first step toward multi-user computing

In the late 1950s and 1960s, a new idea emerged: time-sharing. Instead of one user at a time, the computer rapidly switches its attention between many users — so fast that each user feels they have the computer's full attention.

MIT's Compatible Time-Sharing System (CTSS, 1961) allowed multiple users to connect via dumb terminals — keyboards and screens with no computing power of their own, connected to the central computer by wires. This created a very simple "network": one powerful computer, many terminals radiating out from it like spokes on a wheel.

Time-sharing architecture: Terminal 1 ──────┐ Terminal 2 ──────┤ Terminal 3 ──────┤──── MAINFRAME (does all computing) Terminal 4 ──────┤ Terminal 5 ──────┘ Each terminal: just a keyboard + screen. Zero computing power of its own. The connection is a simple serial wire. All intelligence is at the centre.

This is called a star topology — one central node, everything else connected directly to it. It is simple, but brittle: if the mainframe fails, everyone loses everything. This fragility was exactly what would motivate the invention of the internet.

🧮

Claude Shannon and information theory (1948)

The mathematics that underpins all digital communication
▾

The fundamental questions Shannon asked

In 1948, a Bell Labs mathematician named Claude Shannon published "A Mathematical Theory of Communication" — arguably the most important paper in the history of computing and networking. He asked two questions that nobody had properly formalised before:

1. How much information can a communication channel carry?
2. How efficiently can information be encoded and compressed?

The bit — the atom of information

Shannon gave us the word bit (binary digit). A bit is the smallest possible unit of information: the answer to a yes-or-no question. Either 0 or 1. Either off or on.

Every piece of information — a letter, a number, a sound, an image, a video — can be encoded as a sequence of bits. The only question is how many bits you need.

Why bits? Because the physical medium — electricity in a wire, light in a fiber, radio waves in air — can represent exactly two states reliably: high voltage or low voltage, light on or light off, signal or no signal. Two states = 1 bit. The bit is not arbitrary; it matches the physics of our most reliable signaling technology.

Shannon's channel capacity theorem

Shannon proved mathematically that every communication channel has a maximum rate at which information can be transmitted without errors, given the amount of noise (random interference) in the channel. This is called the Shannon limit or Shannon capacity.

The formula: C = B × log₂(1 + S/N) where C is capacity in bits per second, B is the bandwidth (range of frequencies available), and S/N is the signal-to-noise ratio.

This means: you can always do better by either having more bandwidth (a wider pipe) or less noise. But there is a hard ceiling. No amount of engineering can exceed it. Everything in modern networking — Wi-Fi, 5G, fiber — tries to get as close to this ceiling as possible.

Error correction — what to do when bits get corrupted

Real channels are noisy. A bit sent as 0 might be received as 1 due to interference. Shannon proved that you can make errors arbitrarily rare by adding redundancy — extra bits that do not carry new information, but allow the receiver to detect and correct errors.

For example: if you repeat every bit 3 times (000 = 0, 111 = 1), a receiver who gets "010" can take a majority vote and correctly decode it as 0. This is the basic idea behind all modern error-correcting codes, which are crucial in every network link in existence.

ERA 3

ARPANET — The Network Is Born

1960s–1970s — Packet switching and the birth of a new paradigm
🛡️

The Cold War context — why the internet was funded

RAND, nuclear survival, and ARPA
▾

The strategic problem of 1960

The year is 1960. The United States and Soviet Union are in the Cold War — a tense standoff between two nuclear powers. American military planners faced a terrifying problem: the existing telephone network is a star topology — a small number of central switching offices connect everything. A handful of nuclear bombs on those switching centres would sever all communications.

A RAND Corporation analyst named Paul Baran asked: how do you build a communication network that has no single point of failure? One that can route around damage, like water flowing around obstacles?

Paul Baran's insight — distributed networks

In a series of papers from 1960–1964, Baran described three possible network topologies:

Centralised (star): Decentralised: Distributed: [A] [A]─────[B] [A]─[B]─[C] │ │ │ │ │ │ [B]─[C]─[D] [C]─[D]─[E]─[F] [D]─[E]─[F] │ │ │ │ │ │ [E] [G]─────[H] [G]─[H]─[I] If centre fails: If one node fails: Many paths exist. everything dies some paths survive Very hard to destroy.

Baran argued that a distributed network — where every node is connected to multiple others, with no single centre — would be nearly impossible to knock out with a nuclear attack. Traffic would simply route around the destroyed nodes.

(Note: the popular claim that "the internet was designed to survive nuclear war" is an oversimplification, but Baran's work was genuinely motivated by this concern.)

ARPA — the agency that funded it

After the Soviet Sputnik launch shocked the US in 1957, President Eisenhower created the Advanced Research Projects Agency (ARPA) — now DARPA — to ensure American technological superiority. ARPA funded high-risk, high-reward research that private industry would not touch.

In 1966, ARPA's Information Processing Techniques Office, under J.C.R. Licklider and later Bob Taylor, decided to fund a network connecting universities and research labs — so that expensive computers could be shared and researchers could collaborate. This network would be called ARPANET.

📦

Packet switching — the revolutionary idea at the heart of the internet

Why chopping messages into packets changed everything
▾

The problem with circuit switching for computers

Recall that the telephone network uses circuit switching: a dedicated end-to-end path is established before any communication happens, and it stays reserved for the entire call.

This is terrible for computers. Why? Because computers do not communicate continuously. A computer sending a file is sending large bursts of data separated by silence. If you use circuit switching, you are reserving the entire path even during the silences — wasting capacity that other pairs of computers could be using.

The packet switching idea — independently invented by two people

Paul Baran (1960s) and Donald Davies at the UK's National Physical Laboratory (1965, independently) both arrived at the same revolutionary idea: packet switching.

Donald Davies also coined the word "packet."

The idea:

Break each message into small chunks called "packets." Send each packet independently across the network. Let each packet find its own route. Reassemble them at the destination.

Circuit switching (old): Sender ══════════════════════════════ Receiver ↑ ENTIRE PATH RESERVED ↑ Even during silence. Very wasteful. Packet switching (new): Message = "Hello, world" Packet 1: [Header: To: B, Seq: 1] [Data: "Hell"] Packet 2: [Header: To: B, Seq: 2] [Data: "o, w"] Packet 3: [Header: To: B, Seq: 3] [Data: "orld"] Each packet routed INDEPENDENTLY. May take DIFFERENT paths through network. Reassembled at destination.

Why this is a revolution

Statistical multiplexing: Because packets from many different conversations are all sharing the same wires, the network can be much more efficiently used. When computer A is silent, the wire can carry packets from computers C, D, and E. No dedicated path is wasted.

Resilience: If one router fails, packets can be rerouted around it. No single point of failure.

No setup time: Circuit switching requires setting up a path before you can send anything — a process that could take seconds. With packet switching, you just send. The first packet can arrive before the path is even "established" (because there is no path to establish).

What a packet looks like

Every packet has two parts: a header and a payload (also called the body).

DEST ADDR
Where it's going
SOURCE ADDR
Where it came from
SEQ NUM
Packet number
CHECKSUM
Error detection
PAYLOAD (DATA)
The actual content

The header is like an envelope — it tells the network where the packet came from, where it's going, what sequence it is in, and how to check for errors. The payload is the actual data — the bit of the message contained in this particular packet.

ARPANET goes live — 1969

IMPs, the first login, and the first network crash
▾

The Interface Message Processor (IMP) — the first router

To build ARPANET, ARPA contracted BBN Technologies (Bolt, Beranek and Newman) in Cambridge, Massachusetts. The key hardware they built was the Interface Message Processor, or IMP.

An IMP was a dedicated minicomputer — a Honeywell DDP-516, about the size of a refrigerator — whose only job was to receive incoming packets, look at the destination address, and forward them on the correct outgoing link. This is exactly what modern routers do. The IMP was the first router.

ARPANET, October 1969: UCLA ─────[IMP1]───────[IMP2]───── SRI (Los Angeles) (Stanford Research Institute) │ │ └──────[IMP3]──────[IMP4] UC Santa U Utah Barbara (Salt Lake City) Each IMP connected to its host computer + to other IMPs. Host computers did not need to know about routing — the IMPs handled that invisibly.

The first message — and the first crash

On 29 October 1969, UCLA student Charley Kline attempted to send the word "LOGIN" from UCLA to the Stanford Research Institute across the network. He typed "L" — it arrived. He typed "O" — it arrived. He typed "G" — the remote system crashed.

The first message ever sent on what would become the internet was "LO." Accidentally poetic.

By December 1969, four nodes were connected. By 1972, there were 37 nodes. By 1981, over 200.

What ARPANET introduced

Beyond packet switching, ARPANET established several important precedents:

Email (1971): Ray Tomlinson at BBN wrote the first email program. He chose the @ symbol to separate username from host — a convention that survives to this day.

FTP (1971): File Transfer Protocol — a way to send files between computers on the network.

Telnet (1969): A way to remotely log into another computer over the network and control it as if you were physically there.

ERA 4

Protocol Wars — TCP/IP and the OSI Model

1970s–1980s — How networks agree to talk to each other
📋

What is a "protocol"? From absolute zero

Why machines need agreed rules to communicate
▾

The handshake problem

Imagine two people who want to have a conversation, but one speaks only Tamil and the other only Russian. They cannot communicate — not because they lack a physical channel (they can hear each other fine), but because they have no shared agreement about what sounds mean what.

Computers face the exact same problem. When two computers are connected by a wire, they can send electrical signals to each other. But a signal has no inherent meaning. Both computers need to agree in advance:

— What does a voltage of 5V mean? (Answer: probably 1, but we have to agree first.)
— How long does each bit last? (Answer: we need the same clock speed.)
— How do we know when a message starts and ends?
— What happens if a packet is lost or corrupted?
— How do we identify each computer uniquely?

A protocol is a precise, written-down agreement that answers all of these questions. Both sides must implement the protocol identically. If they do, they can communicate reliably, regardless of what hardware they are built from.

Analogy: When you answer the phone, you say "hello." The caller says "hello, is this [name]?" You say "yes, speaking." This is a protocol — a conventional set of actions both sides follow to establish a voice call. If you picked up and immediately started talking without saying hello, or if the caller said nothing, the protocol would break and communication would fail.

The problem of the 1970s: incompatible networks

By the mid-1970s, several different packet-switching networks existed: ARPANET, the French CYCLADES, the UK's NPL network, early commercial networks. Each used its own protocols. They could not talk to each other.

The challenge was called internetworking: how do you connect networks that use different protocols? This is where the word "internet" comes from — it is short for "internetwork."

🔗

TCP/IP — the protocols that run the internet (1974–1983)

Vint Cerf, Bob Kahn, and the design that beat everything else
▾

The design challenge

In 1973, Vint Cerf and Bob Kahn set out to design a protocol that could connect any two networks, regardless of their underlying technology. A key design principle was what Cerf later called the end-to-end principle: the network itself should be as simple as possible. Intelligence — error correction, ordering, reliability — should live at the endpoints (the computers), not in the middle of the network.

The middle of the network should just do one thing: forward packets. Routers should not need to know what's in the packets. This keeps the network dumb and the applications smart — which makes the network general-purpose.

The original NCP → TCP split

ARPANET originally used a protocol called NCP (Network Control Program). Cerf and Kahn's 1974 paper proposed replacing it with TCP — the Transmission Control Protocol. By 1978, TCP was split into two separate protocols with very distinct jobs:

IP (Internet Protocol) — handles addressing and routing. Each computer gets a unique IP address. IP is responsible for getting a packet from source to destination, hopping through routers. IP is connectionless and unreliable — it makes no promises that packets will arrive, arrive in order, or arrive at all. It just tries its best.

TCP (Transmission Control Protocol) — handles reliability and ordering. TCP runs on top of IP at the endpoints. It numbers all packets, notices when packets are missing, requests retransmission, and reassembles everything in the correct order before handing data to the application. TCP provides the illusion of a reliable, ordered stream even though the underlying IP network is unreliable.

The TCP/IP collaboration: APPLICATION ── "Send this file" │ TCP ─────── breaks into segments, numbers them, handles │ retransmission if any are lost │ IP ──────── adds source + destination IP address to each │ segment (now called a "datagram"), routes it │ independently through the network │ PHYSICAL ─── puts bits on the wire

IP addresses — giving every machine a unique identity

For a packet to be routed to the right destination, every machine on the network needs a unique address. IP addresses are that address system.

IPv4 (1981): a 32-bit number, written as four decimal numbers separated by dots. Example: 192.168.1.1. A 32-bit address means there are 2³² = 4,294,967,296 (about 4.3 billion) possible addresses.

This seemed like enough in 1981. But by the 1990s it was clear that 4 billion addresses would not be enough for the entire world's devices. This eventually led to IPv6 (128-bit addresses, 340 undecillion possibilities — enough to give every atom on Earth's surface its own address).

The DNS — giving addresses human-readable names

Remembering that your favourite website is at 142.250.180.46 is impractical. The Domain Name System (DNS), created in 1983 by Paul Mockapetris, is a distributed database that maps human-readable names (google.com) to IP addresses (142.250.180.46).

When you type google.com in a browser, your computer first asks a DNS server: "What is the IP address of google.com?" The DNS server replies with the IP, and then your computer connects to that IP. DNS is often called the "phone book of the internet."

January 1, 1983 — "Flag Day"

On January 1, 1983, ARPANET switched entirely to TCP/IP. This date is sometimes called the internet's "birthday." From that day forward, TCP/IP was the lingua franca of the internet — and it remains so today.

The OSI Model — the conceptual framework for all networking

Seven layers that explain how any network works
▾

Why layers?

Networking is enormously complex. You need to deal with physical signals, hardware addresses, logical addresses, reliable delivery, session management, data formats, and application logic — all at the same time.

The solution is to divide this complexity into distinct layers, where each layer handles one specific job and provides a clean service to the layer above it. This is called a layered architecture or a protocol stack.

The International Organization for Standardization (ISO) developed the OSI Model (Open Systems Interconnection) in 1984 as a conceptual framework for networking. Even though the internet uses TCP/IP (not OSI protocols), the OSI layers are still the standard way to think about and explain networking.

Think of the OSI model like postal mail. The Physical layer is the road the truck drives on. The Data Link layer is the truck itself (knows about local delivery). The Network layer is the postal address system (nationwide routing). The Transport layer is the "registered mail" service (confirms delivery). The Application layer is the letter you actually read.

Encapsulation — how layers talk to each other

When you send data, each layer wraps the data from the layer above it with its own header (and sometimes a trailer). This is called encapsulation.

Sending a webpage: Application Layer: [HTTP DATA: "GET /index.html"] Transport Layer: [TCP HEADER][HTTP DATA] Network Layer: [IP HEADER][TCP HEADER][HTTP DATA] Data Link Layer: [ETHERNET HEADER][IP HEADER][TCP HEADER][HTTP DATA][ETHERNET TRAILER] Physical Layer: 0110100101100... (bits on wire) At the receiver, each layer STRIPS its header and passes the inner data up to the next layer.

LAN hardware — Ethernet, hubs, switches, and routers

The physical devices that build networks
▾

Ethernet — the protocol that won the LAN

In 1973, Bob Metcalfe at Xerox PARC invented Ethernet — a protocol for connecting computers in a local area (a building or campus). His insight was to use a shared coaxial cable as a "bus" — all machines connected to the same wire.

The problem: if two machines transmit at the same time, their signals collide and are destroyed. Metcalfe's solution was CSMA/CD (Carrier Sense Multiple Access with Collision Detection):

1. Before transmitting, listen (carrier sense) — is anyone else transmitting?
2. If the channel is clear, transmit.
3. While transmitting, keep listening. If a collision is detected, stop immediately.
4. Wait a random amount of time (to avoid both machines retrying at the same instant), then try again.

This is essentially a protocol for taking turns politely on a shared resource. Ethernet (IEEE 802.3) became the dominant LAN technology worldwide.

MAC addresses — hardware identity

IP addresses identify computers logically (they can change). But hardware needs a permanent physical identity too. Every network interface card (NIC) — the hardware component that connects a computer to a network — has a hard-coded MAC address (Media Access Control address): a 48-bit number written in hexadecimal, like 00:1A:2B:3C:4D:5E.

MAC addresses are used for communication within a single local network. IP addresses are used for communication across the internet (between networks). When your packet needs to move from your computer to your router (local), it uses MAC addresses. When it moves from router to router across the world, it uses IP addresses.

Hub vs. Switch vs. Router — three different devices, three different jobs

Hub (Layer 1)
A simple repeater. Receives a signal on one port and broadcasts it out ALL other ports. Every device sees every packet. No intelligence. Obsolete.
Switch (Layer 2)
Learns which MAC address is on which port. Forwards packets ONLY to the port where the destination device lives. Much more efficient than a hub. The standard today for LANs.
Router (Layer 3)
Connects different networks. Reads IP addresses, looks up routing tables, and forwards packets toward the destination. The basic node of the internet. Your home router connects your LAN to your ISP's network.
ERA 5

The World Wide Web — The Internet Becomes Usable

1989–1999 — HTML, HTTP, browsers, and the dot-com era
🌍

The internet vs. the World Wide Web — they are not the same thing

The web is an application that runs on top of the internet
▾

The confusion

People often use "the internet" and "the web" interchangeably. They are different things.

The internet is the physical and logical infrastructure — the cables, routers, and TCP/IP protocols that allow computers worldwide to exchange packets of data. It has existed since the 1970s.

The World Wide Web (WWW) is one specific application that runs on top of the internet. It consists of web pages linked together by hyperlinks, delivered using the HTTP protocol, and displayed using web browsers. It was invented by Tim Berners-Lee in 1989–1991.

Other applications also run on the internet — email (SMTP), file transfer (FTP), video calls (WebRTC), online games — none of these are "the web." The web is just the most visible application.

Tim Berners-Lee at CERN — 1989

Tim Berners-Lee was a British physicist working at CERN (the European nuclear research centre in Geneva). CERN had thousands of researchers who needed to share documents, papers, and results. Each computer used different software and stored documents in different formats. Finding information was a nightmare.

In March 1989, Berners-Lee submitted a proposal titled "Information Management: A Proposal." His boss, Mike Sendall, wrote on it: "Vague but exciting." Berners-Lee proposed a system where documents could be linked to each other by hyperlinks — clickable connections between documents, stored on different computers around the network.

The three inventions that created the web

Berners-Lee invented three things simultaneously:

1. HTML (HyperText Markup Language) — a language for writing web pages. Tags like <b>, <img>, and <a href="..."> describe the structure and links in a document.

2. HTTP (HyperText Transfer Protocol) — a protocol for requesting and receiving web pages. When you click a link, your browser sends an HTTP GET request to a web server, and the server responds with the HTML of the page.

3. URL (Uniform Resource Locator) — a standard address format for every document on the web. https://www.example.com/page.html consists of the protocol (https), the domain name (www.example.com), and the path (/page.html).

On 6 August 1991, Berners-Lee published the first website at info.cern.ch. It described the World Wide Web project itself.

↔️

HTTP — how web pages are requested and delivered

The request-response cycle, methods, and status codes
▾

The client-server model

HTTP is built on the client-server model. There are two kinds of participants:

A client (your web browser) requests resources.
A server (a computer at Google, Amazon, etc.) serves those resources.

The client always initiates. The server always responds. This is called the request-response cycle.

What an HTTP request looks like

GET /index.html HTTP/1.1 Host: www.example.com User-Agent: Mozilla/5.0 (Chrome, etc.) Accept: text/html ← method ← path ← version GET = "please give me this resource" Other methods: POST (send data), PUT (replace), DELETE

What an HTTP response looks like

HTTP/1.1 200 OK Content-Type: text/html Content-Length: 1234 <html> <body>Hello, world!</body> </html> Status codes: 200 = OK (success) 301 = Moved Permanently (redirected) 404 = Not Found (page doesn't exist) 500 = Internal Server Error (server broke)

Ports — how one computer runs many services simultaneously

A server computer might be running a web server, an email server, and an FTP server simultaneously. How does an incoming packet know which service it is for?

The answer is ports. A port is a 16-bit number (0–65535) that identifies a specific service on a computer. Each packet's TCP header contains a destination port number. Well-known port assignments:

Port 80 = HTTP (unencrypted web)
Port 443 = HTTPS (encrypted web)
Port 25 = SMTP (email sending)
Port 22 = SSH (secure remote terminal)
Port 53 = DNS (domain name lookup)

The browser wars and the explosion of the web

In 1993, NCSA Mosaic became the first web browser with a graphical interface that could display images inline with text. By 1994, Marc Andreessen (one of Mosaic's creators) founded Netscape. By 1995, Microsoft released Internet Explorer and the "browser wars" began.

The web grew exponentially: 1 website in 1991 → 10,000 in 1994 → 1 million in 1997 → 17 million in 2000.

🔐

Security and HTTPS — encrypting the web

SSL, TLS, certificates, and why the padlock matters
▾

The eavesdropping problem

HTTP sends everything in plain text. If you buy something online with a credit card, and HTTP is used, anyone who can observe the packets between you and the server — your ISP, anyone on your Wi-Fi network, any router along the path — can read your credit card number.

The solution is encryption — transforming the data into a form that can only be read by the intended recipient.

SSL and TLS — the encryption layer for the web

Netscape developed SSL (Secure Sockets Layer) in 1994–1995. Its successor, TLS (Transport Layer Security), is the current standard. HTTPS = HTTP running on top of TLS.

TLS uses a technique called public-key cryptography:

Each participant has two mathematically related keys: a public key (shared with everyone) and a private key (kept secret). Anything encrypted with the public key can only be decrypted with the private key.

When you connect to a website using HTTPS:
1. The server sends you its public key (wrapped in a certificate signed by a trusted authority).
2. Your browser verifies the certificate is legitimate.
3. Your browser uses the server's public key to encrypt a shared secret.
4. Both sides now share a secret that nobody intercepting the traffic knows.
5. All subsequent communication is encrypted with that shared secret.

The padlock icon in your browser means TLS is active and the connection is encrypted.

ERA 6

The Broadband Age

1990s–2000s — From dial-up to always-on, faster and faster
📻

Dial-up modems — connecting digital computers to analog phone lines

The modem: modulator-demodulator
▾

The incompatibility problem

By the 1980s and early 1990s, most homes had one thing connecting them to the outside world: a telephone line. Telephone lines carried analog voice signals — continuously varying voltages.

But computers produce digital signals — discrete 0s and 1s. These two types of signals are fundamentally incompatible. You cannot put a digital 0-or-1 signal directly onto an analog telephone line and expect it to survive.

The modem — modulator + demodulator

The solution was the modem — a device that modulates (converts) digital signals into analog signals that can travel over telephone lines, and at the other end demodulates (converts back) the analog signal to digital. MOdulator-DEModulator = MODEM.

The modulation technique: the modem generates audio tones at different frequencies to represent 0s and 1s. These tones are in the audible range — which is why dial-up connections made that distinctive screeching sound. The telephone line was designed for voice (300–3400 Hz), and that's exactly what the modem used.

Computer → [Modem] → Phone line → [Modem] → ISP Computer 0101... analog tones ~~~~wave~~~~ 0101... to internet Typical speeds: 1970s-80s: 300 baud (bits/sec) 1991: 9,600 bps (9.6 kbps) 1994: 28,800 bps (28.8 kbps) 1998: 56,000 bps (56 kbps) ← physical limit of phone line

56 kbps was essentially the physical limit of what could be sent through the analog telephone network without distortion. Loading a 5 MB image at 56 kbps would take over 11 minutes.

Broadband — getting off the analog phone network

Broadband refers to any connection that is always-on (no dialing required), much faster than dial-up, and carries data simultaneously with voice (or replaces voice entirely).

Two main technologies won the early broadband race:

DSL (Digital Subscriber Line) — uses the existing copper telephone wire, but at much higher frequencies than voice. The phone line can carry many frequency bands simultaneously; voice uses 0–4 kHz, DSL uses higher frequencies (up to 2.2 MHz for ADSL). Speeds: 512 kbps to 24 Mbps. The 'A' in ADSL stands for Asymmetric — download is much faster than upload, because most people download far more than they upload.

Cable internet — uses the existing coaxial cable infrastructure built for cable television. Cable is a wider channel than telephone wire, allowing much higher data rates. Speeds: 1 Mbps to hundreds of Mbps.

Fiber optic cables — light as the medium

Fiber optic cables carry data as pulses of light through a thin glass or plastic fiber. Because light travels faster than electrical signals in copper and experiences far less attenuation, fiber optic cables can carry vastly more data over much longer distances.

A single fiber strand can carry terabits per second using wavelength-division multiplexing — sending many different colours (wavelengths) of light simultaneously, each carrying a separate stream of data.

The Transatlantic cables today are fiber optic, each carrying ~160 Tbps (terabits per second). Compare that to the first Transatlantic telegraph cable's 0.001 bps effective throughput in 1858.

🗺️

BGP and the internet's backbone — how routing works at scale

Autonomous Systems, BGP, and the structure of the global internet
▾

The internet is not one network — it is thousands

The internet is not a single unified network. It is a collection of thousands of independent networks — ISPs, universities, companies, government agencies — each operating its own routers and managing its own IP address blocks. These independent networks are called Autonomous Systems (AS).

Each AS has a unique AS Number (ASN). For example, Google's AS is AS15169. Reliance Jio's is AS55836. When you load google.com from an Airtel connection in India, your packet travels: your device → Airtel's network (AS) → one or more transit AS's → Google's AS → Google's servers.

BGP — the protocol that glues the internet together

How do routers in different AS's know how to route packets to each other? The answer is BGP (Border Gateway Protocol), defined in 1989.

BGP is the protocol by which Autonomous Systems announce to each other: "I can reach these IP address ranges." Each AS tells its neighbouring AS's which IP prefixes it can deliver to. Those neighbours propagate the information further. Eventually, every router on the internet knows the best path to every IP prefix.

BGP is famously fragile. If someone mistakenly announces the wrong IP prefixes (as Pakistan Telecom did in 2008, accidentally announcing that they owned YouTube's IP range), traffic can be misdirected globally. These events are called "BGP hijacks" and continue to occur.

IXPs — where networks meet physically

Internet Exchange Points (IXPs) are physical locations where many Autonomous Systems connect their networks to each other. Instead of every AS needing a private connection to every other AS, they all connect to the same physical switch at the IXP, and traffic flows between them directly.

Major IXPs: DE-CIX Frankfurt (over 10 Tbps peak), AMS-IX Amsterdam, LINX London, DE-CIX Mumbai. Thousands of networks peer at these locations, dramatically reducing the cost and latency of intercontinental traffic.

The Wireless Revolution

1990s–2010s — Wi-Fi, cellular, and the smartphone era
📶

Radio waves — the physical medium of wireless communication

Frequency, wavelength, and the electromagnetic spectrum
▾

What radio waves are — from absolute zero

When an electric current changes direction rapidly in a wire (an antenna), it creates a disturbance in the electromagnetic field around it. This disturbance propagates outward through space as an electromagnetic wave — what we call a radio wave.

Radio waves are the same physical phenomenon as visible light, X-rays, and microwaves — they are all electromagnetic radiation, differing only in their frequency (how fast they oscillate).

Frequency is how many times the wave oscillates per second, measured in Hertz (Hz). A 1 GHz wave oscillates 1,000,000,000 times per second.

Wavelength is the physical distance between two consecutive peaks of the wave. Frequency and wavelength are inversely related: higher frequency = shorter wavelength.

The electromagnetic spectrum (simplified): Low freq ←──────────────────────────────────→ High freq Long wave ←──────────────────────────────────→ Short wave AM radio FM radio Wi-Fi/5G Visible X-rays Gamma (MHz) (88-108MHz) (2.4-60GHz) light Penetrates Penetrates Weaker Blocked Dangerous walls easily walls less penetration by walls

Frequency bands used in wireless networking

2.4 GHz (used by Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, microwave ovens): Long wavelength (~12.5 cm). Penetrates walls and obstacles well. But also used by many other devices — very congested.

5 GHz (used by Wi-Fi 5 and Wi-Fi 6): Shorter wavelength. Much more bandwidth available — faster speeds. But shorter range and worse wall penetration.

60 GHz (used by Wi-Fi 6E/7, some 5G): Very short wavelength (~5 mm). Enormous bandwidth — multi-gigabit speeds. But almost no wall penetration; only useful at short distances.

Sub-1 GHz (used by LTE, older cellular): Very long wavelength. Travels for kilometres, penetrates buildings well. Less bandwidth but good coverage. Rural 4G uses these bands.

Encoding data in radio waves — modulation

To carry data on a radio wave, you vary (modulate) some property of the wave:

Amplitude Modulation (AM): vary the height (amplitude) of the wave. Simple, but sensitive to noise.
Frequency Modulation (FM): vary the frequency slightly. More noise-resistant than AM.
Phase Modulation (PM): vary the phase (the starting point of the wave cycle).
QAM (Quadrature Amplitude Modulation): combine amplitude and phase changes. Modern Wi-Fi uses 1024-QAM — encoding 10 bits per symbol by using 1024 distinct combinations of amplitude and phase.

🛜

Wi-Fi — wireless Ethernet

IEEE 802.11 standards, channels, and how Wi-Fi evolved
▾

What Wi-Fi is

Wi-Fi is simply Ethernet without the wires — the same data link layer protocol, but using radio waves instead of copper. Formally, Wi-Fi is a set of standards maintained by the IEEE (Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers) under the designation IEEE 802.11.

The Wi-Fi Alliance (a trade group) brands these standards with friendly names. The evolution:

1997
IEEE 802.11 original — 2 Mbps maximum. Barely practical.
1999
802.11b — 11 Mbps, 2.4 GHz. The first widely adopted Wi-Fi standard.
2003
802.11g — 54 Mbps, 2.4 GHz. Mass adoption begins.
2009
802.11n (Wi-Fi 4) — up to 600 Mbps. Introduced MIMO: multiple antennas.
2013
802.11ac (Wi-Fi 5) — up to 3.5 Gbps, 5 GHz. Beamforming: focus signal toward client.
2019
802.11ax (Wi-Fi 6/6E) — up to 9.6 Gbps. OFDMA: multiple clients simultaneously.
2024
Wi-Fi 7 (802.11be) — up to 46 Gbps, uses 2.4/5/6 GHz simultaneously.

MIMO — Multiple Input, Multiple Output

A single antenna can only send/receive one stream of data at a time. MIMO uses multiple antennas — both at the router and at the device — to send multiple independent streams simultaneously. A 4×4 MIMO system has 4 transmit and 4 receive antennas, potentially quadrupling throughput.

This works because of a phenomenon called multipath: radio waves bounce off walls and arrive at the receiver from multiple directions at slightly different times. In older systems, multipath caused interference. MIMO uses mathematical algorithms to separate the different paths and use each one as a separate data stream.

📱

Cellular networks — from 1G voice to 5G data

Cells, base stations, handoff, and the mobile internet
▾

Why "cellular"?

A single powerful radio transmitter could cover a large area — but every call would have to share the same frequency spectrum, severely limiting capacity. The cellular concept (developed at Bell Labs in the 1960s–70s) solved this by dividing a geographic area into small hexagonal zones called cells.

Each cell has a low-power base station at its centre. Neighbouring cells use different frequencies. But cells that are far enough apart can reuse the same frequencies without interfering. This frequency reuse dramatically multiplies the total capacity of the system.

Cellular frequency reuse: [A]─────[B]─────[C] / \ / \ / \ [B] [C][A] [B][C] [A] ← same letter = same frequency \ / \ / \ / [C]─────[A]─────[B] Adjacent cells use different frequencies. Distant cells can reuse same frequency. More cells = more capacity (but more infrastructure).

Handoff — moving between cells

When you walk or drive, you move from one cell to another. The system must hand your call or data session from one base station to the next without dropping it. This is called handoff (or handover). Your phone constantly measures signal strength from nearby base stations, and the network coordinates the transfer so seamlessly that you don't notice.

The generations of cellular

1G (1979)
First generation. Analog voice only. No data. Easily intercepted — no encryption.
2G (1991)
Digital voice (GSM standard). Encryption. SMS text messages. GPRS added basic data (9.6–115 kbps).
3G (2001)
Real mobile internet: 384 kbps to ~7 Mbps (HSPA). Enabled smartphones and mobile browsing.
4G/LTE (2009)
True broadband mobile: 10–150 Mbps typical. Enabled streaming video, video calls, app stores.
5G (2019)
Up to 20 Gbps peak, 1 ms latency. Millimetre-wave for ultra-high density, sub-6GHz for coverage.
ERA 8

Cloud, SDN, and the Modern Internet

2000s–present — virtualisation, content delivery, and software-defined everything
☁️

Content Delivery Networks — bringing the server closer to you

Why geography matters and how CDNs solve it
▾

The speed of light problem

Even at the speed of light, distance matters. A packet from Mumbai to New York takes roughly 80ms just in propagation delay (the time for light to physically travel ~13,000 km). Round-trip: 160ms. Any interactive application — gaming, video calls, real-time web — suffers noticeably above 50–100ms.

If Netflix's servers were only in California and you streamed a movie in Bengaluru, every packet would cross the Pacific Ocean and come back. The solution is obvious: put copies of the content closer to users.

CDN architecture

A Content Delivery Network (CDN) is a geographically distributed network of servers — called edge servers or Points of Presence (PoPs) — that cache (store copies of) content close to users.

When you load netflix.com, DNS returns not the IP of Netflix's origin server in California, but the IP of the nearest CDN edge server — maybe one in Mumbai or Hyderabad. That edge server has cached the video you want. Your packets never leave India.

Major CDN operators: Cloudflare (270+ cities), Akamai (1300+ cities), AWS CloudFront, Fastly. Cloudflare alone handles ~20% of global web traffic.

NAT — sharing one IP among many devices

Remember that IPv4 has only ~4 billion addresses? There are far more than 4 billion connected devices. The solution (a stopgap until IPv6 takes over) is NAT (Network Address Translation).

Your home router has one public IP address (assigned by your ISP). Inside your home, devices get private IP addresses (like 192.168.1.x) that are not globally routable. When your laptop makes a request, the router:

1. Replaces your laptop's private source IP with the router's public IP.
2. Records this translation in a table.
3. When the response comes back, the router translates the destination back to your laptop's private IP and forwards it.

This allows millions of private networks to share the limited IPv4 address space. The trade-off: NAT breaks the end-to-end principle, since two devices behind different NATs cannot easily connect to each other directly (which is why online gaming and video calls need clever techniques like STUN and hole-punching).

⚙️

Software-Defined Networking (SDN) and virtualisation

Separating the control plane from the data plane
▾

The traditional router's two jobs

Traditional routers combine two functions:

Data plane (forwarding): actually moving packets from input port to output port, at line speed. This must be extremely fast — hardware-based.

Control plane (routing): deciding which path packets should take. Running BGP, OSPF, and other routing protocols. Computing and updating the routing table.

In traditional networking, these two functions are bundled together in the same physical box, using proprietary software from vendors like Cisco or Juniper.

SDN — separating control from forwarding

Software-Defined Networking (SDN), formalised around 2008–2011 at Stanford, separates the control plane from the data plane:

— Physical forwarding hardware (switches/routers) becomes simple and fast — just follows instructions.
— A centralised SDN controller (running on standard servers) has a complete view of the entire network and pushes forwarding rules to all the hardware via a standardised protocol (OpenFlow).

This makes networks programmable — you can change routing policies by updating software on the controller, without touching any physical hardware. Google's B4 network (connecting its global data centres) has been SDN-based since 2012 and achieves near-100% link utilisation, compared to ~30–40% in traditional networks.

Virtual networks — networking without hardware

Cloud providers (AWS, Google Cloud, Azure) now implement entire networks in software. When you create a Virtual Private Cloud (VPC) on AWS, you are not getting any physical networking hardware — AWS creates a software simulation of switches, routers, firewalls, and load balancers, running on commodity servers.

Technologies like VXLAN (Virtual Extensible LAN) tunnel virtual network packets inside regular IP packets, so the virtual network is completely isolated from the physical one. Many thousands of customers can share the same physical hardware while each experiencing completely separate, logically isolated networks.

🔬

The modern internet — what happens when you load a webpage

Tracing every step from keypress to pixels on screen
▾

Every step, from the beginning

You type https://www.google.com and press Enter. Here is exactly what happens:

Step 1 — DNS lookup: Your OS checks its local cache: do I already know Google's IP? If not, it asks your router's DNS resolver, which asks a recursive resolver (your ISP's or 8.8.8.8), which climbs the DNS hierarchy: root server → .com nameserver → Google's authoritative nameserver → returns 142.250.180.46.

Step 2 — TCP handshake: Your computer sends a SYN packet to 142.250.180.46:443. Google's server responds with SYN-ACK. Your computer responds with ACK. A TCP connection is established. This takes one round-trip time (RTT) — ~10ms if Google's CDN is nearby.

Step 3 — TLS handshake: Your browser and Google's server negotiate which cipher suites to use, exchange certificates, and establish a shared encryption key. This takes 1–2 more RTTs. After this, all data is encrypted.

Step 4 — HTTP request: Your browser sends an HTTP GET request over the encrypted TLS connection: "GET / HTTP/2 Host: www.google.com"

Step 5 — Routing: Your request leaves your laptop, goes to your Wi-Fi router (Layer 2), is NAT'd and sent to your ISP (Layer 3), travels through several routers (BGP routing), reaches a Google IXP connection or edge server (CDN), and arrives at a Google data centre.

Step 6 — Server processing: Google's load balancers distribute your request to one of thousands of web servers. The server generates or serves cached HTML. The response is sent back through the same network path.

Step 7 — Browser rendering: Your browser receives the HTML, parses it, discovers linked resources (CSS, JavaScript, images), makes additional HTTP requests for each, assembles the page, and renders pixels on screen.

Total time from Enter key to first pixels: typically 200–500ms for a first visit, 50–200ms for a cached/CDN-nearby site.

🚀

Where networking is going — HTTP/3, QUIC, satellite, and beyond

The ongoing evolution continues
▾

QUIC and HTTP/3 — rethinking transport

TCP, invented in the 1970s, has a fundamental inefficiency for web browsing: if any one packet is lost, the entire stream stalls while waiting for retransmission, even if other parts of the web page could be rendered. This is called head-of-line blocking.

Google developed QUIC (Quick UDP Internet Connections) as a replacement for TCP for web traffic. QUIC is built on UDP (a simpler, unreliable transport), adds reliability and security in user-space, and crucially: multiple streams within one connection are independent. If one stream loses a packet, other streams are not blocked.

HTTP/3, standardised in 2022, is HTTP running on QUIC. It is now used for ~25% of web traffic.

Low Earth Orbit satellites — internet from space

Traditional satellite internet used geosynchronous satellites at 35,786 km altitude — far enough to stay above one spot on Earth. The round-trip signal time is ~600ms — too high for many applications.

Starlink (SpaceX), OneWeb, and Project Kuiper (Amazon) are deploying thousands of satellites at 550–1200 km altitude. At this altitude, round-trip time drops to 20–40ms — comparable to ground-based internet. This brings broadband to remote areas with no terrestrial infrastructure: rural India, the Amazon, the Arctic.

5G and edge computing

5G's most transformative feature is not just speed — it is ultra-low latency (target: 1ms) and massive device density (up to 1 million devices per km²). This enables new categories: autonomous vehicles communicating in real time, industrial robots, remote surgery, augmented reality that doesn't lag.

Edge computing places computing resources (servers) at or near the cell towers and base stations — not in central data centres. When an autonomous vehicle needs to make a split-second decision, it cannot afford 50ms to reach a cloud server. But a nearby edge server, 2ms away, can respond fast enough.
