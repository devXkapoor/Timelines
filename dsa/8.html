Era VIII · Making "Fast Enough" Reliable

Heaps, Hashing, and Self-Balancing Trees

Three structures that close the gaps left open by earlier eras — a tree that's always balanced no matter how you insert into it, a way to get array-like O(1) access for data that isn't numbered 0 to n, and a structure purpose-built for "give me the smallest/largest, fast."

The shift in this era. Look back at the open threads from earlier eras. Era IV introduced the binary search tree, with its excellent O(log n) operations — but flagged that this only holds if the tree stays "balanced," and that bad insertion orders can degrade it to O(n). Era VI's Dijkstra's algorithm needed a "priority queue" — something that always hands you the current-smallest item, efficiently — without specifying how to build one. And throughout, "look something up by name/key" (find a person's record by their ID, look up a word's definition) has been implicitly assumed to be easy, without ever explaining how it can be O(1) when the "name" isn't a small integer like an array index.

This era closes all three gaps. Each is a different instance of the same underlying ambition: take an operation that "should" be fast based on what we've learned about complexity, and design a structure that guarantees it — not just "usually," but provably, for every input, including deliberately adversarial ones.

1962 · Georgy Adelson-Velsky & Evgenii Landis, USSR

The AVL Tree — A Binary Search Tree That Fixes Itself

+
The Source
The AVL tree (named after its inventors) is a binary search tree that, after every insertion or deletion, checks whether the tree has become "unbalanced" — specifically, whether the heights of any node's two subtrees differ by more than 1 — and if so, performs a local restructuring operation called a "rotation" to restore balance, before continuing.
Vocabulary
Balance factorfor a given node, the difference between the height of its left subtree and the height of its right subtree. An AVL tree requires this difference to always be -1, 0, or +1 for every node.
Rotationa local rearrangement of a small number of nodes (typically 2-3) that changes the tree's shape (which node is "above" which) while preserving the binary search tree property (left-smaller, right-larger) and the set of values stored. Rotations are the mechanism by which balance is restored.
Self-balancinga data structure that automatically maintains some "good shape" guarantee through its own update operations, without requiring a separate, periodic "fix everything" pass.
Amortizeddescribing a cost that might vary per-operation, but averages out to a fixed bound over a sequence of operations. Rotations don't happen on every insertion, but the AVL tree's guarantees are framed so that, on average (and even in the worst single case here), operations remain O(log n).
First Principles

Recall the open problem from Era IV: insert values 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 (in that order) into a binary search tree, and you get a tree that's really just a linked list in disguise — every node has only a right child, height = n, and "O(log n) search" becomes O(n) search. The binary search tree's speed guarantee was conditional on "the tree happens to be balanced" — but nothing in the basic insertion procedure ensures that.

The AVL tree's insight: after a normal insertion (which might create an imbalance), don't just stop — check whether any node's balance factor has gone out of range (beyond ±1), and if so, perform a rotation to fix it locally. A rotation can be visualized as "promoting" a child node to take its parent's place, with the parent becoming a child instead — a small, local rearrangement of pointers (recall: nodes are connected via pointers, just like linked lists from Era IV) that doesn't require touching the rest of the tree, but changes the height balance of the affected subtree.

Inserting 10, 20, 30 in order would normally create a degenerate, linked-list-like chain. A rotation promotes 20 to the root, with 10 and 30 as its children — restoring the balance property and reducing height from 3 to 2.

The crucial guarantee: by maintaining the balance condition (every node's subtree heights differ by at most 1) after every single insertion or deletion, the AVL tree mathematically guarantees its height is always O(log n) — not "usually," not "for typical inputs," but for every possible sequence of insertions and deletions, including ones an adversary deliberately constructs to try to break it. This is the qualitative leap from Era IV's plain binary search tree: a guarantee rather than a hope.

This pattern — "perform the obvious operation, then do extra local work to restore some invariant that guarantees good behavior" — is extremely common in advanced data structures. The specific details of rotations matter less, for your purposes, than this general shape: a self-balancing structure trades a small amount of extra work on every update for a guarantee that holds in the worst case, permanently. Many other self-balancing structures exist (red-black trees, B-trees, splay trees, treaps), each with different specific rules for "what counts as balanced" and "how to restore it," but all following this same shape. For most CP purposes, you won't implement these from scratch — most languages provide a built-in balanced-tree-based structure (often called a "sorted set" or "sorted map," or implemented via a tree underneath a "map"/"dictionary" type) — but understanding why they offer guaranteed O(log n) operations, and what operations they support (insert, delete, search, and often "find the smallest element ≥ X" — a very useful query), is essential.

Why this matters for CP "Ordered set/map" structures (built on self-balancing trees) are a staple intermediate tool — they support insertion, deletion, and lookup all in O(log n), and let you efficiently find the smallest element greater than X, or the largest element less than X (operations a plain hash-based structure, covered next, cannot do efficiently, because hashing deliberately discards ordering information). Recognizing "I need to maintain a changing collection AND repeatedly ask order-related questions about it" is the signal to reach for one of these.
1953 onward · Hans Peter Luhn and others, IBM and beyond

Hash Tables — O(1) Lookup for Non-Numeric Keys

+
The Source
A hash table stores key-value pairs by applying a "hash function" to each key — a function that converts the key (which might be a string, a tuple, or any other kind of data) into a number — and using that number as an index into an underlying array. Two different keys that happen to produce the same index ("collide") are handled via one of several collision-resolution strategies.
Vocabulary
Hash functiona function that takes an input of (potentially) any size or type and produces a fixed-size output (usually an integer), deterministically — the same input always produces the same output. A good hash function spreads different inputs roughly evenly across possible outputs.
Key / valuein a hash table, data is stored as key-value pairs — the "key" is what you look things up by (e.g., a username), the "value" is the associated data (e.g., that user's account information).
Collisionwhen two different keys produce the same hash value (and therefore the same array index). Since the number of possible keys is usually far larger than the array size, collisions are inevitable and must be handled.
Bucket / chainingone common collision-resolution strategy: each array slot ("bucket") holds a small linked list (a "chain") of all key-value pairs that hashed to that slot. Looking up a key means hashing it, then searching the (hopefully short) chain at that slot.
Load factorthe ratio of (number of stored items) to (number of buckets). As load factor grows, chains get longer and performance degrades — so hash tables typically "resize" (allocate a larger underlying array and re-distribute everything) once load factor crosses a threshold.
Average-case O(1)hash table operations are O(1) on average, assuming a reasonably good hash function and load factor — but, importantly, NOT guaranteed O(1) in the worst case (the next callout explains why this matters).
First Principles

Recall Era IV's array: O(1) access via address = base + index × size — but this requires the "index" to be a small, sequential integer (0, 1, 2, ..., n-1). What if what you actually want to look up by isn't a small integer — it's a person's name, a word, a coordinate pair, an arbitrary string? The hash table's idea: convert the arbitrary key into a number (via the hash function), and use array-style indexing on that number. "Alice" might hash to 7, "Bob" might hash to 2 — and now you can use the same O(1) array-access trick, just with an extra translation step first.

"Bob" and "Eve" both hash to index 2 — a collision. Both are stored in a chain at bucket 2; looking up "Eve" means hashing to 2, then checking the short chain there.

The "average-case O(1)" qualifier is essential, and worth dwelling on, because it's a genuinely different kind of guarantee from anything in Era VIII's first entry. If the hash function distributes keys roughly evenly, chains stay short (close to 1 item each on average), and lookups are effectively O(1). But if many keys happen to hash to the same bucket — whether by bad luck, a poorly-designed hash function, or (in adversarial settings) a deliberate attack exploiting a known hash function — chains can grow long, and lookups degrade toward O(n), exactly like searching an unsorted linked list. This is why "average-case O(1), worst-case O(n)" is the honest description of a hash table, in contrast to the AVL tree's "guaranteed O(log n), no exceptions."

In competitive programming specifically, this distinction has a sharp practical edge: some languages' default hash implementations have had known weaknesses where carefully-constructed inputs cause many collisions, degrading an expected-O(1)-per-operation solution to O(n) per operation — turning an intended O(n) or O(n log n) solution into O(n²), which can time out. This has occasionally been exploited by problem setters specifically to catch naive hash-based solutions. The lesson generalizes beyond this specific gotcha: "average case" is doing real work in that phrase — it's not a minor caveat, and understanding when a structure's average-case guarantee might not hold is part of using it responsibly.

Despite this caveat, hash tables (often called "hash maps," "dictionaries," or "hash sets" depending on language and whether values are stored) are used constantly in CP — for counting occurrences of items, checking "have I seen this before?", and memoization (Era VII) when the "state" is too complex or sparse to index directly into an array (e.g., the state is a tuple of several values, or the relevant values are huge numbers — a direct array would need billions of slots, almost all unused, but a hash map only stores entries for states actually encountered).

Connecting back: arrays, trees, and hash tables, side by side Three ways to store key-value-like data, each with a different tradeoff: arrays (O(1) access, but keys must be small sequential integers known in advance), self-balancing trees (O(log n) for everything, guaranteed, AND maintains sorted order — supports "find nearest" queries), hash tables (O(1) average for lookup/insert/delete, arbitrary key types, but no ordering information and no worst-case guarantee). Choosing between the latter two is one of the most common "which structure do I actually need here?" decisions in CP: if you need order-based queries, a tree-based structure; if you just need fast existence/lookup/counting and don't care about order, a hash-based one.
1964 · J. W. J. Williams, England (introduced alongside heapsort)

The Heap — A Tree That's Only Sorted "Vertically"

+
The Source
A (binary) heap is a binary tree with two properties: it's "complete" (every level is fully filled, except possibly the last, which fills left-to-right with no gaps), and it satisfies the "heap property" — every node's value is less than or equal to (for a "min-heap") or greater than or equal to (for a "max-heap") the values of its children. Note that, unlike a binary search tree, there's no left/right ordering rule — only a parent/child rule.
Vocabulary
Heap propertyevery parent is ≤ both its children (min-heap) or ≥ both its children (max-heap). This says nothing about how the two children compare to each other, or about any ordering between "left" and "right" — a crucial difference from binary search trees.
Complete binary treea binary tree where every level is fully filled except possibly the last, and the last level fills from left to right with no gaps. This shape constraint means a heap can be stored compactly in a plain array (explained below), without needing pointers at all.
Priority queuean abstract data type (recall this term from Era IV) supporting "insert an item with a priority" and "remove and return the item with the highest (or lowest) priority" — a heap is the standard way to implement this efficiently.
Sift up / sift down (bubble up/down)the operations that restore the heap property after an insertion (sift up: move a new element up past parents it's smaller than) or removal (sift down: move a displaced element down past children it's larger than).
Heapsorta sorting algorithm that builds a heap from the input, then repeatedly removes the maximum (or minimum) element, achieving O(n log n) — guaranteed, with no bad-input worst case unlike quicksort, and in-place unlike merge sort.
First Principles

This entry directly resolves the open thread from Era VI: Dijkstra's algorithm needed "repeatedly give me the unvisited vertex with the smallest tentative distance, efficiently" — a priority queue. The heap is the structure purpose-built for exactly this.

Compare the heap property to the binary search tree's property (Era IV). A BST says: left subtree < node < right subtree — a globallocal and vertical: parent ≤ children (for a min-heap) — nothing is said about left vs. right, and nothing is said about how far-apart nodes compare to each other (a node's grandchild could be smaller than its sibling, for instance). This weaker constraint is deliberate: it's exactly strong enough to guarantee that the root is always the overall minimum (or maximum) — which is the one specific query a priority queue needs to answer instantly — while being weak enough to restore quickly after changes.

A min-heap: 3 ≤ 7 and 3 ≤ 5 (root ≤ children) ✓. 7 ≤ 9 and 7 ≤ 8 ✓. Notice 5 (right child of root) is smaller than 7, 9, and 8 elsewhere in the tree — the heap property says nothing about cross-branch comparisons, only parent-child.

The "complete binary tree" shape constraint has a beautiful consequence: because every level is fully packed (except possibly the last, filled left-to-right), you can store the entire tree in a plain array, with no pointers at all — node at array index i has children at indices 2i+1 and 2i+2, and a parent at index (i-1)/2 (integer division). This is a direct callback to Era IV's array-vs-pointer-structure discussion: the heap gets the conceptual benefits of a tree shape, but the memory-efficiency and cache-friendliness of a plain array, precisely because its shape is constrained enough to be predictable.

Insertion: add the new element at the end of the array (the next position in the complete-tree shape), then "sift up" — repeatedly swap it with its parent as long as it's smaller than its parent (for a min-heap), until the heap property is restored. Removal of the minimum: it's always the root (array index 0) — remove it, move the last element of the array into the root position, then "sift down" — repeatedly swap it with whichever child is smaller, until the heap property is restored. Both operations are O(log n), because the tree's height is O(log n) (it's complete — same height-bound reasoning as a balanced BST, but guaranteed automatically by the shape constraint, with no rotations needed).

This gives Dijkstra's algorithm (Era VI) its practical implementation: maintain a min-heap of (tentative distance, vertex) pairs. Repeatedly remove the minimum (O(log n)) to get the next vertex to finalize, and whenever a distance is updated during relaxation, insert the updated (distance, vertex) pair (O(log n)). With V removals and up to E insertions total, the overall complexity becomes O((V + E) log V) — a concrete, implementable complexity bound for an algorithm that, back in Era VI, was described only in terms of "repeatedly pick the smallest."

Era VIII in summary, and what's still open Three structures, three different "fast, guaranteed" properties: AVL/balanced trees guarantee O(log n) for ordered operations (insert, delete, search, AND order-based queries like "next smallest"). Hash tables give average-case O(1) for unordered key-based lookup, with an honest worst-case caveat. Heaps give O(log n) insert/remove-min, with O(1) peek-at-min, implemented compactly as an array thanks to the complete-tree shape constraint — directly enabling efficient Dijkstra's algorithm and any "always process the current best item next" pattern. Together with Era IV's arrays/lists/stacks/queues and Era V-VI's sorting/searching/graph traversal, this completes what's often called the "core toolkit" of DSA — the structures and algorithms that essentially every intermediate CP problem draws on in some combination.
