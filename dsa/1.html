Era I · The Long Prelude

Before the Word "Algorithm" Existed

How humans first learned to write down step-by-step instructions — and why every data structure and contest problem you'll ever touch traces back to this.

Where we're starting, and why. Competitive programming today feels like a world of its own — ratings, online judges, contest formats, problem tags. But every single idea underneath it (a "loop," a "step," an "input," a "proof that this always works") is not a computer science invention. These ideas are thousands of years old, and they were invented by people solving very ordinary problems: dividing land, calculating taxes, predicting eclipses, settling debts.

This first era covers the period before any machine existed — when "algorithm" simply meant "a method written down clearly enough that someone else could follow it without you standing next to them." That single idea — a process so precisely described that it can be handed to someone (or something) else — is the seed of everything: data structures, complexity, correctness proofs, and yes, eventually, Codeforces.

c. 2000–1600 BCE · Babylon

Clay Tablets and the First Written "Algorithms"

+
The Source
Babylonian scribes wrote mathematical problems on clay tablets using cuneiform script, in the form: "I did such-and-such to a number, and got this result. What was the number?" — followed by a numbered sequence of operations to find the answer, ending with the phrase "this is the procedure."
Vocabulary
Cuneiformone of the earliest writing systems — wedge-shaped marks pressed into soft clay using a cut reed (a hollow plant stem), then dried or baked hard. "Wedge-shaped" means shaped like a narrow triangle, like the tip of a doorstop.
Scribea person whose profession was writing — at the time, a rare and highly trained skill, since most people could not read or write.
Procedurea fixed sequence of steps to follow to get a result. This is the oldest ancestor of what we now call an "algorithm."
Reciprocalin mathematics, the reciprocal of a number is 1 divided by that number (e.g., the reciprocal of 4 is 1/4). Babylonians used precomputed tables of reciprocals to turn division into multiplication.
First Principles

Imagine you need to solve the same kind of math problem hundreds of times — say, dividing harvested grain fairly among workers, or calculating how much land tax someone owes. You could re-derive the answer from scratch every single time, thinking hard each time. Or — and this is the leap — you could notice that the steps you take are always the same, regardless of which numbers you plug in. Once you notice that, you can write the steps down once, and anyone (even someone less skilled than you) can follow them.

That is the entire idea of an algorithm: separating the steps from the specific numbers. The Babylonians did exactly this. Their tablets show worked examples — "if the number is 7, do this, then this, then this, and you get the answer" — but the pattern of steps was meant to be reused for any starting number, not just 7.

They also built lookup tables — for multiplication, for reciprocals, for square roots — so that instead of recalculating something every time, you could just look it up. This is the ancestor of one of the most important ideas in all of computer science: trading memory for computation. If you can store an answer once, you don't have to recompute it. (You will meet this idea again, much later, as "memoization" and "dynamic programming" — two of the most common techniques in competitive programming. The core idea — don't redo work you've already done — is over 3,500 years old.)

Why this matters for DSA later Every time you write a lookup table, a cache, or a "precompute this once" array in a competitive programming solution, you are using the same trick a Babylonian scribe used with a clay reciprocal table: storing an answer so the steps don't need to be repeated.
c. 1650 BCE · Egypt

The Rhind Papyrus — Multiplication Without a Times Table

+
The Source
The Rhind Mathematical Papyrus describes a method of multiplying two numbers using only doubling and addition: to multiply A by B, repeatedly double A (1×A, 2×A, 4×A, 8×A, ...) while keeping track of which doublings, when added together, give B copies of A — then sum only those rows.
Vocabulary
Papyrusa paper-like writing material made from the pith (the soft inner part) of the papyrus plant, which grew along the Nile river.
Doublingrepeatedly adding a number to itself: 5 → 10 → 20 → 40, etc. Each step is "times two."
Binarya number system using only two digits, 0 and 1, where each position represents a power of two (1, 2, 4, 8, 16...). You don't need to know binary yet — just notice that "doubling repeatedly" and "binary" are deeply connected, which will matter enormously later.
First Principles

Here's the problem the Egyptians faced: they had no memorized multiplication tables like "7 × 8 = 56." So how do you multiply two large numbers using only the two operations you're confident about — doubling and adding?

Example: multiply 13 × 24.

Write down doublings of 24: 1×24=24, 2×24=48, 4×24=96, 8×24=192. Now notice that 1 + 4 + 8 = 13. So 13 × 24 = 24 + 96 + 192 = 312. You never multiplied — only doubled and added.

Why does this matter? Because 13 in binary is 1101 — meaning 13 = 8 + 4 + 1. The Egyptian method is, without anyone calling it that, binary decomposition: breaking a number into a sum of powers of two. This single idea — that any number can be built from powers of two, and that doubling is a "cheap" operation — becomes the foundation of:

• How computers represent every number internally (binary).
• "Fast exponentiation" / "binary exponentiation," a famous competitive programming technique for computing huge powers (like 2^1000000) quickly — by repeatedly squaring instead of repeatedly multiplying.
• Binary search, binary trees, and the general idea of "halving the problem each time," which is the heart of O(log n) algorithms.

A thread to remember "Repeatedly halving or doubling something is dramatically faster than doing it one step at a time" is an idea you will see again and again — in binary search, in fast exponentiation, in segment trees, in merge sort. It starts here, with people who simply didn't have a multiplication table.
c. 300 BCE · Alexandria, Greece

Euclid's Algorithm — The Oldest Algorithm Still Used Today

+
The Source
In Book VII of his "Elements," Euclid describes a procedure for finding the greatest common measure of two numbers: repeatedly subtract the smaller number from the larger, until the two numbers become equal — that shared value is the greatest common measure.
Vocabulary
Greatest Common Divisor (GCD)the largest number that divides evenly into two given numbers, with nothing left over. Example: the GCD of 12 and 18 is 6, because 6 is the biggest number that divides both exactly.
Greatest Common Measurethe ancient Greek term for the same idea as GCD — but they thought of it geometrically, as the largest length that could measure two different lengths a whole number of times.
Remainderwhat's "left over" after division. If you divide 17 by 5, you get 3 remainder 2 (because 5×3=15, and 17−15=2).
Modulo (mod)a mathematical operation that gives you the remainder of a division. "17 mod 5 = 2." You will see this symbol constantly in competitive programming, often as % in code.
Iteration / Iterativedoing the same step over and over, each time using the result of the previous step, until some stopping condition is reached.
First Principles

This is, genuinely, one of the most important moments in this entire history — and you will use this exact algorithm, or something built on it, constantly in DSA and CP.

The problem: given two numbers, find the largest number that divides both of them evenly. Why would anyone care? Originally: dividing land into equal square plots, simplifying fractions, or scheduling things that repeat at different intervals.

Euclid's insight: if you have two lengths, and you keep cutting the longer one down by the length of the shorter one, eventually they become equal — and that final equal length is the answer. In modern arithmetic terms (this is the "subtraction" version):

GCD(48, 18): 48 − 18 = 30 → GCD(30, 18): 30 − 18 = 12 → GCD(18, 12): 18 − 12 = 6 → GCD(12, 6): 12 − 6 = 6 → now they're equal (6, 6). Answer: 6.

Centuries later, this got optimized using the remainder instead of repeated subtraction — instead of subtracting 18 from 48 over and over, you can jump straight to the remainder: 48 mod 18 = 12. Then 18 mod 12 = 6. Then 12 mod 6 = 0. When the remainder hits 0, the other number is the GCD. This "modulo" version is the one taught and used today, and it's breathtakingly fast — it can handle numbers with hundreds of digits in a handful of steps.

Why is this the single most important entry so far? Because it is the first algorithm in history that we can prove always terminates (stops) and always gives the correct answer — and Euclid actually wrote out that reasoning. This is the birth of two ideas that define computer science:

1. Correctness — not just "this works on the examples I tried," but "here is a logical argument for why this must always work, for any input."

2. Termination — a guarantee that the process actually finishes, rather than running forever. (This sounds obvious, but it becomes a genuinely hard and important question once you start writing real algorithms — entire branches of theory exist around "does this program ever stop?")

Looking ahead Euclid's Algorithm is the textbook first example of recursion and iteration — the idea that a problem can be solved by reducing it to a smaller version of the same problem (GCD(48,18) becomes GCD(18,12), which is "the same kind of problem, but smaller"). This "shrink the problem, solve the smaller one, repeat" pattern is the single most important pattern in all of DSA — it underlies recursion, divide-and-conquer, and a huge fraction of CP problems.
c. 240 BCE · Cyrene / Alexandria

The Sieve of Eratosthenes — Finding Primes by Elimination

+
The Source
To find all prime numbers up to some limit, write out all numbers from 2 to that limit. Starting from 2, cross out every multiple of 2 (other than 2 itself). Move to the next number not yet crossed out, and cross out all of its multiples. Repeat. The numbers never crossed out are the primes.
Vocabulary
Prime numbera whole number greater than 1 that has no divisors other than 1 and itself (e.g., 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13...). Numbers that aren't prime are called "composite."
Sievea tool with mesh or holes used to separate larger objects from smaller ones — like a kitchen strainer. Here it's a metaphor: the "mesh" lets composite numbers fall through (get eliminated), leaving primes behind.
Multiplea number you get by multiplying another number by a whole number. Multiples of 3 are 3, 6, 9, 12, 15...
Brute forcea term you'll hear constantly in CP — solving a problem by trying all possibilities directly, without any clever shortcut. The opposite of an "elegant" or "optimized" algorithm.
First Principles

Suppose you want to know: is 91 prime? The brute-force way is to try dividing 91 by every number from 2 up to 90 and check if any divide evenly. That's slow, and if you wanted to find all primes up to, say, 1,000,000, doing this check individually for every number would take an enormous amount of time.

Eratosthenes' insight was to flip the question around. Instead of asking, for each number, "is this prime?" — he asked, for each prime found so far, "which numbers can I now rule out?" Once you know 2 is prime, you instantly know that 4, 6, 8, 10, 12... are not prime (they're all multiples of 2) — without checking each one individually. Then 3 is prime, so 6, 9, 12, 15... are out. By the time you've processed a few small primes, huge swaths of numbers are already eliminated, and what's left must be prime.

This is a profound shift in thinking that comes up constantly in DSA: instead of asking the same question repeatedly for each item, find a way to answer it for many items at once. This is sometimes called "batch processing" the problem, and it's the difference between an algorithm that takes seconds and one that takes hours on large inputs.

The Sieve of Eratosthenes, in essentially its original form, is still one of the first things taught in any competitive programming course today, because problems involving primes are extremely common, and the sieve can find all primes up to 10 million in well under a second on a modern computer.

A preview of "complexity" Checking if one number N is prime by brute force takes roughly N steps. Checking every number up to N this way takes roughly N×N steps — far too slow for large N. The Sieve does the same job for all numbers up to N in roughly N×log(log(N)) steps — a number so close to N that it's considered "almost free." This gap — between "do it for each item separately" and "do it for all items together, smartly" — is the entire reason complexity analysis (Big-O notation, which you'll meet in a later era) was eventually invented: to describe and compare exactly this kind of difference.
c. 820 CE · Baghdad, House of Wisdom

Al-Khwārizmī — The Man Whose Name Became "Algorithm"

+
The Source
Muḥammad ibn Mūsā al-Khwārizmī wrote a book describing systematic methods for solving linear and quadratic equations using a small set of standard operations — and a separate work that introduced the Hindu-Arabic numeral system (including the digit zero) to the Arabic-speaking and, later, European world.
Vocabulary
Linear equationan equation where the unknown value appears only to the first power, like 2x + 3 = 11 (no x², no x³).
Quadratic equationan equation involving the unknown squared, like x² + 5x + 6 = 0.
Hindu-Arabic numeralsthe digits 0–9 and the "place value" system we use today (where the position of a digit determines whether it means ones, tens, hundreds...). This replaced older systems like Roman numerals, which had no zero and were terrible for calculation.
Algorism / Algorithm"Algorism" was the medieval European word for "the art of calculating with Hindu-Arabic numerals," derived from a Latinized form of al-Khwārizmī's name. Over centuries, "algorism" slowly morphed into the modern word "algorithm" — and broadened from meaning specifically "calculation with these digits" to meaning "any well-defined step-by-step procedure."
House of Wisdoma major intellectual center in Baghdad during this period, where scholars translated, studied, and built upon Greek, Persian, and Indian texts in mathematics, astronomy, and other sciences.
First Principles

Two separate contributions came from this period, and both matter enormously, for different reasons.

First: the number zero and place-value notation. This sounds almost too basic to be important, but consider trying to do long multiplication in Roman numerals — multiplying XLVII by XXIII — there's no clean mechanical procedure; you basically have to "think" your way through it using clever tricks. With 0–9 and place value (where "203" means 2 hundreds, 0 tens, 3 ones), multiplication and division become mechanical — a fixed procedure you can follow without cleverness, just by moving digits around according to fixed rules. This is the first time arithmetic itself becomes something you could, in principle, hand to a machine (or an unskilled person) and get a guaranteed-correct answer. Representation — how you write numbers down — directly determines what procedures are even possible. This idea — that the right representation makes an algorithm possible, and the wrong one makes it nearly impossible — will reappear constantly when you study data structures: the same data, stored differently, can make an algorithm trivial or nearly impossible.

Second: the word itself. Al-Khwārizmī's name, transliterated into Latin over centuries through translation and copying, slowly became "algorismus," then "algorism," and eventually "algorithm." The meaning also drifted — from "the specific method of calculating with these new digits" to, eventually, "any precise step-by-step procedure for solving a class of problems." By the time this word reaches the 20th century, it means exactly what it means to you today.

The throughline so far Notice the pattern across this entire era: Babylonians (procedures + lookup tables), Egyptians (binary decomposition via doubling), Euclid (proof of correctness and termination, recursive shrinking), Eratosthenes (batch elimination instead of individual checks), al-Khwārizmī (representation determines what's computable, and the name "algorithm" itself). None of these people had a computer. All of these ideas are sitting, fully formed, inside almost every solution you will eventually write on Codeforces. The next era is where these ideas start crystallizing into formal mathematics and logic — the bridge toward machines.
