Era III · From Theory to Metal

The First Computers and the Birth of "Programming"

Theory becomes hardware, instructions become memory, and "programming" becomes a thing that humans do — with its own emerging vocabulary of variables, loops, and subroutines.

The shift in this era. By the end of Era II, every theoretical ingredient was in place: a precise definition of "algorithm," proof that a single machine could run any program if given the right instructions, and the conceptual separation between "the machine" and "the instructions." What was missing was simply a physical machine. Driven largely by the urgent calculation needs of the Second World War (code-breaking, artillery trajectory tables), that machine got built — multiple times, in multiple places, within about a decade.

But building the machine immediately created a new, very practical problem that nobody had needed to think about before: how do you actually tell it what to do? This era is where "programming" stops being a thought experiment and becomes a job — with its own emerging tools, its own headaches, and its own first attempts at making the process less painful. Several of the basic vocabulary words you'll use constantly in DSA — variable, memory address, subroutine, bug — are born here.

1945 · Philadelphia, USA

ENIAC — A Machine "Programmed" by Rewiring

+
The Source
ENIAC (Electronic Numerical Integrator and Computer) was a large electronic computer built primarily to calculate artillery firing tables. To change what calculation ENIAC performed, operators had to physically rearrange cables on a large plugboard and set hundreds of switches — a process that could take hours or days.
Vocabulary
Electronicworking via the flow of electricity through components like vacuum tubes (glass tube devices that can switch electrical signals on and off, the predecessors to transistors), rather than via moving mechanical parts like gears. Electronic operations happen far faster than mechanical ones.
Firing tablestables used by artillery crews to determine the angle and settings needed to hit a target at a given distance, accounting for factors like wind and altitude — calculating these by hand was extremely slow and was a major driver of early computer development.
Plugboarda board with many sockets, into which cables are plugged to create specific electrical connections — similar in concept to old telephone switchboards. Rearranging the cables changes how signals flow through the machine.
Hardwiredwhen a machine's behavior is fixed by its physical wiring/construction, rather than by separate instructions it reads. ENIAC's "program" was, in this sense, built into its physical configuration.
First Principles

ENIAC was electronic — built from vacuum tubes rather than mechanical gears — and was thousands of times faster than any mechanical calculator. But notice what it did not yet have: the clean separation between "the machine" and "the instructions" that Babbage and Lovelace imagined, and that Turing's Universal Machine formalized. To run a different calculation on ENIAC meant physically rewiring it — the "program" and "the machine's physical structure" were still, in an important sense, the same thing.

This wasn't a step backward — ENIAC was an enormous practical achievement, and its speed was revolutionary. But it highlighted, in very concrete terms, exactly the gap that needed closing: instructions needed to become something the machine could read, the same way it reads data — stored in the same kind of memory, changeable without touching the physical wiring. This is precisely the problem the next entry solves, and it's worth sitting with why it matters: if "the program" requires physically rewiring the machine, then every new task is, in effect, building a new machine. Only once the program becomes data the machine reads does "writing a program" become a distinct activity from "building a machine" — and only then can "programming" become a discipline of its own, separate from electrical engineering.

Why this matters for DSA later The distinction between "configuration baked into the machine" versus "instructions read as data" is the ancestor of a distinction you'll meet constantly: code versus data. In DSA, you'll see this distinction blur in interesting ways — for example, a function can be passed around and stored in a data structure just like a number can (this is the "functions as data" idea from Church's lambda calculus in Era II) — but the baseline assumption, that instructions live in memory and can be read, written, and moved like any other data, starts here, with ENIAC representing the "before" picture.
1945 · Princeton, USA

The Stored-Program Concept — Instructions Become Data

+
The Source
In a widely circulated report describing the design of a new computer (EDVAC), John von Neumann described an architecture in which both the program's instructions and the data it operates on are stored together in the same memory, in the same format — and the machine reads instructions from memory one at a time, executes them, and proceeds to the next, with the ability to jump to different points in the stored instructions based on conditions.
Vocabulary
Memorya large array of numbered storage locations ("addresses"), each capable of holding a value. Think of it as a long row of numbered mailboxes, each able to hold one piece of information.
Addressthe number identifying a specific location in memory — like a house number on a street. "Memory address 1024" refers to a specific mailbox, regardless of what's currently inside it.
Stored-program computera computer whose instructions are held in the same memory as its data, and can therefore be read, and even modified, the same way data is. This is the architecture nearly every computer in existence uses today.
Fetch-execute cyclethe basic repeating process of a stored-program computer: fetch (read) the next instruction from memory, execute (carry out) it, then move to the next instruction (usually the next one in memory, unless the instruction says to jump elsewhere) — and repeat, forever, until told to stop.
Conditional jump / branchan instruction that says, in effect, "if some condition is true, continue execution from a different memory address instead of the next one." This is the machine-level ancestor of if statements and loops.
First Principles

This is, arguably, the single most important practical idea in the entire history of computing — and it's worth slowing down on it, because nearly every concept in DSA assumes this architecture as a backdrop.

The key idea: memory is just a long sequence of numbered slots. Some of those slots happen to hold data (numbers you're computing with) and some happen to hold instructions (steps of the program) — but memory itself doesn't know or care which is which. Whether a particular slot is "data" or "instruction" depends entirely on how it's used — specifically, whether the machine's "current instruction pointer" is pointing at it (in which case it's read as an instruction) or whether some other instruction reads it as a value to compute with (in which case it's data).

The machine repeats a simple cycle forever: look at the memory address the "instruction pointer" currently points to, read what's there, carry it out, and then normally move the pointer to the next address — unless the instruction itself was a "jump," in which case the pointer moves to wherever the jump says. This "unless" is everything: it's what makes loops possible (jump backwards to repeat a block of instructions) and what makes if/else possible (jump past a block of instructions, or not, depending on a condition).

Now connect this to data structures. An array — a sequence of values stored contiguously (next to each other), each accessible by an index/position — is, quite literally, just a chunk of this memory, viewed as a sequence of numbered slots, exactly the way von Neumann's memory model works. "Array index 5" and "memory address (start + 5)" are, at the hardware level, the same idea. This is why arrays have the performance properties they do (instant access to any position, since it's just "compute an address and look there") — and why other data structures, like linked lists (which you'll meet later), which don't sit contiguously in memory, behave so differently.

Also connect this to recursion (Euclid, Era I; lambda calculus, Era II). For a function to call itself, the machine needs to remember "where to return to" after the inner call finishes — and it does this by storing that return address in memory, in a structure called a "call stack" (which you'll meet properly later, but the name should already start to feel familiar if you've heard of "stack overflow" errors — that's literally this structure running out of memory).

The single sentence to remember "A computer is a machine that repeatedly reads a number from memory, treats it as an instruction, carries it out (possibly jumping to a different memory location next), and repeats" — almost everything else in DSA is built as increasingly elaborate structure on top of this one simple loop. Variables are named memory locations. Arrays are contiguous memory locations. Loops are jumps backward. Recursion is jumps that remember where to come back to. Every data structure is, underneath, just a particular pattern of organizing this flat sequence of numbered slots.
Late 1940s–1950s · UK & USA

Assembly Language and the First Subroutines

+
The Source
Early programmers wrote instructions directly as raw numbers (machine code). To make this more manageable, "assembly languages" were developed, using short mnemonic text abbreviations (like ADD, JUMP, LOAD) in place of numeric codes, with a translator program ("assembler") converting the text into the actual numbers the machine required. Around the same period, programmers developed the technique of writing a reusable block of instructions once and "calling" it from multiple places in a program — the subroutine.
Vocabulary
Machine codethe raw numeric instructions a specific computer's hardware directly understands and executes — the most basic, lowest-level form of a program.
Mnemonica memory aid — typically a short word or abbreviation chosen because it's easier to remember than the thing it represents. "ADD" is easier to remember than "the number 00100000."
Assembly languagea programming language that's a thin, human-readable layer directly on top of machine code — each assembly instruction typically corresponds to exactly one machine instruction, just written with mnemonics instead of raw numbers.
Assemblera program that translates assembly language (text with mnemonics) into machine code (raw numbers) that the hardware can execute directly.
Subroutinea named, reusable block of instructions that can be "called" (jumped to) from various points in a program, and which "returns" back to wherever it was called from when finished. The direct ancestor of what we now call a "function" or "method."
Call stacka structure (built on ordinary memory) that keeps track of "where to return to" each time a subroutine is called — especially important when subroutines call other subroutines, or call themselves (recursion), since the machine needs to unwind these calls in the correct reverse order.
First Principles

Two separate-but-related improvements happened here, both motivated by the same underlying problem: writing programs as raw sequences of numbers is exhausting, error-prone, and makes it nearly impossible to reuse work.

Assembly language addresses the "raw numbers are unreadable" problem. Note carefully what assembly language is not: it's not a fundamentally different way of thinking about computation — it's a direct, one-to-one stand-in for machine code, just written with words instead of numbers. This is the first appearance of a pattern that recurs throughout the rest of computing history: build a translator program that lets humans write in a more convenient notation, and have the translator convert it to whatever the machine actually needs. Every programming language you'll ever use — Python, C++, Java — is, at the end of a chain of translation steps, eventually turned into machine code. Assembly language is the first link in that chain.

The subroutine addresses a different problem: reuse. Before subroutines, if you needed to perform the same sequence of steps (say, "compute a square root") at five different points in your program, you had to write out those steps five separate times. The subroutine idea says: write it once, give it a name/location, and at each of the five points, instead of repeating the steps, write a single instruction that means "go run that block, then come back here when you're done."

The "come back here when you're done" part is the subtle, crucial bit — and it's why the call stack exists. If subroutine A calls subroutine B, and B calls subroutine C, then when C finishes, the machine needs to return to B (not to A, and not to wherever A was originally called from). The call stack handles this by behaving like a physical stack of papers: each time a subroutine is called, a note ("return here when done") is placed on top of the stack; each time a subroutine finishes, the note on top is read and removed, telling the machine where to go next. This "last in, first out" behavior — the most recently added note is the first one removed — is your first real encounter with a named, general-purpose data structure (the stack), which you'll study formally in a later era, but which is already doing real work here, silently, every time a subroutine returns.

A direct line to recursion Recall Euclid's algorithm (Era I) — GCD(48,18) reduces to GCD(18,12), which reduces to GCD(12,6). If you implement this as a subroutine that calls itself (recursion), each call pushes a new "return here" note onto the call stack. GCD(48,18) calls GCD(18,12) — push a note. That calls GCD(12,6) — push another note. Eventually the recursion stops, and the notes get popped off one at a time, returning control back up the chain in reverse order. The "stack" data structure and the concept of recursion are two sides of the same coin — one is the mechanism, the other is the technique that uses it.
1947–1952 · USA

Grace Hopper and the First Compiler

+
The Source
Grace Hopper and her team developed one of the first compilers — a program that translates instructions written in a higher-level notation (closer to mathematical or English-like expressions) into machine code, automating much of the translation work that programmers previously did by hand. Hopper's team also documented one of the earliest literal uses of the term "bug" to describe a hardware fault, and "debugging" to describe the process of finding and fixing it.
Vocabulary
Compilera program that translates source code written in one programming language (typically a "higher-level," more human-friendly language) into another language (typically a "lower-level" one, like machine code or assembly) — usually translating the entire program at once, before it runs.
High-level languagea programming language designed to be relatively readable and convenient for humans, abstracting away many details of the specific hardware it will run on. "High" refers to being further from the hardware, "low" refers to being closer to it.
Source codethe human-written/human-readable version of a program, before any translation has happened.
Bugan error or flaw in a program (or, originally, in hardware) that causes it to behave incorrectly. "Debugging" is the process of finding and removing such errors.
First Principles

If assembly language was "give instructions mnemonic names instead of raw numbers, but keep a strict one-to-one correspondence with machine code," the compiler is the next, much bigger step: "let programmers write in a notation that's convenient for expressing ideas, and have a program figure out the (often much longer and more intricate) sequence of machine instructions needed to actually carry that idea out."

This is a genuinely different kind of translation. One line of a high-level language might correspond to dozens of machine instructions, and the "best" translation might depend on context in ways that require the compiler to do real analysis — not just substitution. This is the beginning of programming languages as we recognize them today: notations designed around how humans think about problems, with the (substantial) burden of mapping that down to hardware shouldered by the compiler.

This matters enormously for your DSA learning, for a very practical reason: when you write for i in range(n): or int arr[100];, you are writing in a notation designed for expressing algorithmic ideas clearly — and a compiler (or interpreter) handles the translation to the fetch-execute-cycle, memory-address world of Era III's second entry. The entire reason you can think in terms of "arrays," "loops," and "functions" rather than raw memory addresses and jump instructions is because of this layer. But — and this is the crucial point for understanding performance, complexity, and why some operations are "fast" and others "slow" — the underlying hardware reality (memory addresses, the fetch-execute cycle, the call stack) doesn't go away. It's still there, underneath, and it's why array access is fast and certain other operations are slow. Learning DSA well means being able to mentally move between these layers: think in the high-level notation for clarity, but understand the low-level reality for performance.

On "bugs": the word's persistence (it's universal in programming today) reflects something true about programming as an activity from the very start — programs, even short ones, very often don't do what their author intended on the first try, and a huge fraction of programming time is spent figuring out why. This isn't a sign of carelessness; it's an inherent feature of precisely specifying complex step-by-step processes. In competitive programming specifically, you will spend a notable fraction of contest time debugging — and the practice of "tracing through your algorithm by hand on a small example" (which you'll do constantly) is a direct descendant of how early programmers found bugs: stepping through the machine's state, instruction by instruction, by hand.

Era III in one sentence By the early-to-mid 1950s: machines exist that store programs as data (von Neumann), programmers can write in mnemonics instead of raw numbers (assembly) and reuse code via subroutines and a call stack (a real data structure already at work), and compilers exist that let programmers write in higher-level notations while a translator handles the mapping to hardware. The stage is now fully set for the next era's big leap: once writing programs is this much easier, people start asking how to organize data itself — and "data structure" as a formal concept, plus the question "how do we measure whether one algorithm is better than another?", become the central questions. That's Era IV, and it's where Donald Knuth enters the story.
